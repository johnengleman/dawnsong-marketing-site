## Source of truth: the product feeling

The product's actual screens are a brand source of truth. If this document ever describes a brand that does not feel like the current app, the document is stale.

Sona should feel **warm, soft, illustrated, emotionally safe, and quietly alive**. The app expresses this through watercolor landscapes, cream surfaces, deep forest-green type, rounded cards, gentle progress charts, and the hedgehog mascot. Those choices are not decoration; they communicate the core promise that the user can return without being punished.

Marketing, copy, account branding, AI-generated images, App Store screenshots, and TikTok slideshows must preserve that feeling. A technically correct hook is off-brand if it makes Sona feel cold, harsh, sterile, shame-driven, or like a generic productivity dashboard.

## The name

The app is **Sona** — written as one word. The name should feel warm, calm, and easy to return to. It carries the promise of a gentle companion for real life: progress that keeps going after imperfect days.

> **Spelling.** The wordmark is **Sona**. It is always written as one word, with an initial capital in product and marketing copy.

> **Naming note.** In all brand and marketing contexts — copy, hooks, bios, hero lines, this doc — the wordmark is **Sona**.

## The one-sentence brand

**Sona helps you build an extraordinary life through long runs of returning — not perfect streaks.**

Returning is the practice. Showing up after a miss is the entire point.

## The brand idea

We use *several* marketing slogans and angles — different hooks for different TikToks, slideshows, and audiences. That variety is good; it keeps content fresh. But every slogan must point back to **one** idea, or the marketing stops feeling like one product.

### The one idea

> **Sona lets you keep going — because it feels safe to return after being human.**

This is the thing every screen, slogan, hook, and video is really about. It is not itself a slogan — it's the idea *underneath* the slogans. You don't put it on a screen; you check screens against it.

### The three values we stack

Sona does everything a normal productivity brand does — real progress, real follow-through, real results — and then **stacks three values on top: kindness, peacefulness, and optimism.** That stack *is* the brand. Strip the three values away and we're just another productivity account.

Two clarifications this section exists to enforce:

- **Sell the transformation, never the features.** Every piece of marketing is about *who the viewer becomes* — the calm, capable, follow-through person they've always wanted to be. The routine system is the vehicle, never the subject. The moment a slogan or slide is just listing what the product does, it's off-brand.
- **We are not anti-hustle, and not pro-laziness.** Hustle is how meaningful outcomes actually get reached, and we say so. We are pro-progress, pro-neatness, pro-tidiness, pro-follow-through — never glorify slacking. The *only* difference between us and the grind crowd is the emotional fuel: we work hard *from* kindness, optimism, and peace — calm instead of panicked, kind instead of self-punishing. "Gentle" describes the posture, not the output.

### The north star: a guardian angel, and beauty as a differentiator

This is the emotional and visual center of everything — the picture every TikTok, screen, and image should be checked against.

**Beauty is a primary differentiator, not the wrapper.** The apps our user has tried didn't only fail on mechanics (the punishing streak reset). They also failed because they were *ugly* — cluttered, clinical, complex, exhausting to look at. Nobody becomes their best self in a fluorescent-lit waiting room. Sona wins on beauty *equally* with the mechanic: it should be something you genuinely **want** to look at and live inside. "Beautiful" is a load-bearing brand claim, not a nice-to-have.

**The metaphor: heaven reaching down a hand. Sona is the user's guardian angel.** It does two things at once for someone who wants to change their life:

1. Gives them something beautiful to look at — a *dream* of the life they want, made visible. Gorgeous, luminous, "I want to live there."
2. Reaches a hand down to help them get there — kind, encouraging, *actionable* guidance, spoken in the voice of a guardian angel.

The three values (kindness, peace, optimism) are exactly what an angel's voice sounds like — this metaphor is the home they were missing. The product itself is *"the most beautiful thing your guardian angel could build to help you become who you want to be."*

**The formula for any post:** a dreamlike, gorgeous image (the life they want) **+** a kind, optimistic, actionable word from the angel (one step toward it). Dream to look at; hand to climb with.

**Who this is for:** people who want to live surrounded by beauty — a beautiful home, beautiful days — *and* become a better version of themselves, and who want the *becoming itself* to feel beautiful, like heaven, not like punishment.

**The kind of beauty we mean: cozy, not grand.** This is the most important guardrail. "Beautiful" here is always **cozy, warm, gentle, watched-over** — a sunlit cottage, a reading nook, misty golden hills, a candle-and-tea desk. The constant is the *feeling*: golden light, grace, being cared for. It is **never** epic, cold, sublime, or marble-and-cathedral "heaven." If an image feels grand or cold instead of cozy and warm, it's wrong.

Two visual registers, same cozy feeling:
- **The app UI** stays in the **cozy watercolor** world — cream paper, forest-green type, sage/honey/dusty-blue palette, the hedgehog. The live app screens are the source of truth here.
- **Marketing aspirational imagery** (TikTok dream-images, brand references) may be **photorealistic and dreamlike** — think the enchanted, golden, magical-realm look of an elvish Lothlórien: soft god-rays, glowing dust motes, ethereal warmth. Photoreal is allowed and encouraged for these *as long as it stays cozy and inviting*, not grand. Reference set: `/sona-marketing/brand/north_star_refs/photoreal/`.

**Guardrails:**
- Keep "angel / heaven" as felt **light, grace, and beauty** — not literal halos, churches, or religious iconography, and never greeting-card saccharine.
- Angels help you *act*. They don't tuck you in for a nap. The "do the work, become better" spine stays — beauty must never tip into pretty-but-lazy (see "The three values we stack" above).

> Visual reference set defining "the beauty we mean" lives in `/sona-marketing/brand/north_star_refs/`.

### How we say it: lead with the problem, close with the destination

We have two main lines, and they do two different jobs. Don't mix them up.

- **Open with the problem — "Stop restarting the same goal."**
  This is the line for *cold attention*: App Store, ad and video hooks, bios. "Goal" is useful here because it is the user's pain-language, not because every product control should be labeled as a goal. People react to their pain faster than to a promise, so the problem goes first.

- **Close with the destination — "Make your life extraordinary."**
  This is the *payoff*, not the hook. It only works *after* the user believes — at the **end** of onboarding, in brand slideshows, on App Store screenshot #1. **Never on the paywall, never in push notifications, never as a cold opener.**

The onboarding already does this: Screen 1 opens on the restart loop, and the closing bridge points to the destination.

### Varying the hooks

Individual TikToks and slideshows can lead with different angles — that's encouraged. Common ones:

- The problem — "Stop restarting the same goal."
- Treated like a human — "A system that treats you like a human." *(avoid "habit tracker"/"habit app" — see the vocabulary rules below.)*
- Overwhelm — "For when life is too much for a perfect streak."
- Identity — "Become someone who follows through."

These can be worded however works for the content. They stay cohesive because they all point back to the one idea.

### The test

Before shipping any new slogan or hook, ask one question: **does this point back to "keep going, not punished for being human" — and does it eventually meet the user at the streak-reset enemy?** If yes, it's on-brand even if it's worded nothing like the others. If no, cut it.

## What the brand stands against

The single mechanic Sona exists to kill: **the counter that visibly resets to zero on a missed day.**

We are *not* against the feeling of momentum, of a chain of days building up — that dopamine is real and the product delivers it through the pebble grid and consistency score. What the brand rejects is the *punishing reset*: the mechanic that turns one missed day into a moral event and teaches users to quit.

So the brand is **anti-*punishing*-streak, not anti-streak.** This distinction governs the whole marketing posture (see "On the word 'streak'" below).

## Audience

**Primary viewer:** Someone who has tried every habit app, planner, streak, reset, Notion dashboard, and "new Monday" motivation. **They are not lazy. They are tired of starting over.**

**Their internal monologue:**

- "I know what I need to do. I just do not stay with it."
- "I keep saying I will start next week."
- "I hate that this is still the goal I keep restarting."
- "I know the goal I need. I just don't stay with it."
- "I am great at starting, terrible at continuing."
- "One missed day ruins the whole thing for me."
- "I do not trust myself to follow through anymore."

**What they want:**

- To become someone who keeps promises to themselves.
- To stop feeling like their desired outcomes are always vague future plans.
- To turn those outcomes into goals they can actually return to.
- To build a calmer, more stable life — without the panic and shame of hustle *culture* (they still want to work hard; they just want to do it from a calm place, not a frantic one).
- To feel supported by the system, not judged by it.

**Audience skew:** The brand may skew slightly toward women in its warmth, softness, goal/reset language, and visual identity, but it should not become exclusive or stereotyped. The target is anyone who wants gentler structure, self-trust, and a system that feels emotionally safe enough to return to.

## Positioning stack

The core argument, ordered broad → concrete. Marketing content walks down this stack.

1. **Life-level fear** — "What if another year passes and this outcome is still sitting there?"
2. **Opportunity cost** — "20 minutes a day becomes 122 hours in a year."
3. **Broken system** — "Streak apps make one missed day feel like failure."
4. **The reframe** — "A missed day is a dip, not a reset."
5. **Identity promise** — "Become someone who follows through."
6. **Product proof** — routine builder, consistency score, pebbles, flexible rhythms, area-level progress.

**The big principle:** Don't sell "habit tracking." Sell the feeling of finally giving an important outcome a calm system you can keep returning to. The viewer should think: *"If I don't try this, I'm probably going back to the same restart loop."*

## Voice and tone

**Use:**

- Calm urgency
- Peer-to-peer language — written by someone who has the same problem, not an expert talking down
- Honest self-recognition
- Regret prevention *without* accusation
- "System" language, not "discipline" language
- First person for short-form scripts
- Lowercase for slide text where the account style fits
- Warm specificity — concrete goal details, objects, and moments that feel lived-in
- Emotional safety — the user should feel recognized before they feel challenged

**Avoid:**

- "you are wasting your life"
- "fix your laziness"
- "never fail again"
- "accomplish everything instantly"
- "crush your outcomes"
- "discipline hack"
- Clinical language (unless a specific piece is deliberately for that audience)
- Cold optimization language that makes the app feel like a dashboard instead of a place to return
- Dark, harsh, masculine-coded discipline branding as a default

**The litmus test:** *Could a Stoic, a Zen practitioner, a craftsperson, and a parent all read this and nod?* If yes, you're on-brand. If only the Stoic nods, you've drifted toward *toxic* hustle culture — the panicked, shaming kind. (Hard work itself is on-brand; the panic and shame around it are not.)

## Vocabulary rules

We use the *user's* vocabulary, not our own. The audience speaks *streaks, habits, routines, goals, progress, consistency,* and larger life outcomes — we use those words. What we redefine is the *mechanic underneath*: progress is scored by consistency, not binary perfection; it survives a missed day.

### On areas, routines, and outcomes

- **Area** is the primary product noun in app UI. An area is a purpose container: health, morning, evening, learning, fitness, recovery, sleep, writing. It can hold goals, routines, or simple practices without forcing the user to name which kind.
- **Habit** is the repeated action inside an area. Habits can be daily, weekly, or monthly, but they are still in service of the area.
- **Goal** is useful in emotional marketing and user-authored names, but avoid making it the default label for every product control.
- **Outcome** is the larger desire or life change. It is useful in emotional marketing, but product UI and App Store positioning should not make outcomes feel like the main object the app manages.
- A user can still have a standalone habit, but the brand should imply that the powerful version of Sona is building a calm routine system, not collecting isolated checkboxes.

### On the word "streak"

This word is handled with precision:

- **Use "streak" to name the old, broken thing** the user is escaping — "the streak that resets on you."
- **Do NOT use "streak" to describe what Sona gives the user.** That's a **"run"**, a **"long run of returning"**, a **"consistency score."** The name rejects a streak; the product offers a run. The two must never contradict.
- Streak *vocabulary, energy, milestones, and visual chains* are all on-brand for marketing — the audience speaks streak, and we meet them there. We're anti-*punishing*-streak, not anti-streak.

### The "habit tracker" rule

**Escape the category in marketing; stay findable in search.**

- **"Habit"** (the noun — the user's own word) is fine everywhere. "Build the habit that finally sticks."
- **"Habit tracker"** (the category word — the meta-noun of failed competitors) is **banished from user-facing marketing**: TikTok, slideshows, cat videos, bios, ads, hero headlines, brand copy, and the preferred visible App Store subtitle. Replace with positioning language ("routine builder," "a tool for the long run," "a calm system for finally following through," "the app for people who quit habit apps").
- **"Habit tracker" is KEPT** in App Store keywords, long description, and paid-search bidding — that's where buyers actually search. Removing it everywhere would kill organic App Store discovery.
- **App Store title** balances: one category word + positioning flavor.
- **Preferred App Store subtitle direction:** "Guilt-free habit routines" or "Habit & routine builder." Start with the version that reads clearest in App Store search results; avoid "tracker" unless conversion data forces it.

### Competitor names

No competitor *names* in scripts — no "Streaks app," no "Habitica" by name. Streak *vocabulary* is fine and encouraged.

## Product proof points (the differentiators)

Three differentiators, in priority order. In short-form video these appear in **screen recordings only — never named in spoken script.**

- **A — Consistency over streaks.** A scored consistency percentage that absorbs misses without resetting. The pebble/history grid visibly demonstrates forgiveness — missed days are part of the chain; the chain continues. Calibrated against the **90% bar**. *(Primary differentiator — most content showcases this.)*
- **B — Area-based routine system.** Areas are the first-class organizing object in the app UI. Each area can include daily, weekly, and monthly habits, with an area-level consistency view.
- **C — Rest periods.** A first-class rest affordance: declared rest doesn't break anything, no consecutive-period rests, auto-resume at period end. Sickness, travel, and hard weeks are designed for, not patched around. *(Shipped.)*
- **D — Flexible cadence.** Daily / weekly / monthly habits, each living at its natural rhythm inside the area. Not everything is a daily checkbox.

## Visual identity

Full reference: [DESIGN.md](DESIGN.md). Summary:

**Register:** painterly-atmospheric — calm, dignified, soft-edged, contemplative, warm, illustrated, and emotionally safe. The mental model is *"what would the painter who made the onboarding landscapes do?"* — not *"what would a UX designer do?"* The handoff bar: a user moving from onboarding into the app should feel "same app, same artist, same world."

The current product screens are the practical reference: a warm watercolor header with the hedgehog mascot, cream glassy cards, deep forest-green headings, muted blue/green progress marks, rounded controls, soft mountain/path metaphors, and progress shown as a gentle landscape rather than a cold report card.

**Core principles:**

- **Soft over sharp** — squircle corners, atmospheric shadow falloff, hairline-or-no borders, subtly textured surfaces.
- **Warm over cool, muted over saturated** — warm cream surfaces, deep forest-green text, earned peach/gold accents. When a screen feels off, the fix is almost always *reduce saturation / warm the palette*, not add a color.
- **Quiet typography** — Nunito (headings, `700` not `800/900`), Inter (body/numbers), Playfair Display (hero numbers). Polish weights and spacing, don't change fonts.
- **Quiet motion** — soft cubic easing, 300–500ms, subtle press-scale. Celebration is *recognition*, not spectacle.
- **Emotional safety over pressure** — visuals should lower shame and make return feel possible. Even performance/progress surfaces should feel supportive, not evaluative.
- **Illustrated warmth over sterile productivity** — the brand can use charts, app screens, and data, but they must stay in the watercolor/cream/forest-green world.

**Hard "don't"s:** no hard drop shadows, no bright/candy accents, no confetti or bold celebratory animation, no decorative ornament, **no streak-app energy** (red X's, broken-chain warnings, "you're about to lose your progress"), no childish playfulness, no Edo-Japan literalism (we borrow the watercolor *register*, not the motifs), no generic discipline-bro minimalism, no cold SaaS dashboard feel.

**Mascot:** The hedgehog mascot is **kept** — a hard-won, recognizable brand asset. It is being *restyled* into the soft-watercolor register (loose diffused edges, warm muted palette, visible paper texture), not retired. It appears across the app, in marketing, and on onboarding screens — including emotional-anchor screens where it adds warmth or emotional anchoring. (The earlier "paintings carry the onboarding moment alone" rule was retired on 2026-05-22: paintings alone read as static on screens that don't have one.) Character-level rules: [DESIGN.md](DESIGN.md) (Part 4 — Mascot Art Direction).

**The 90% bar, visually:** the load-bearing product promise. A trophy icon in earned gold (`colors.goldDark`) appears at ≥90%, nothing below. It's a *recognition mark* that the user is in the practice — never a scary target.

## Brand decision checklist

When making a brand, copy, or marketing decision, ask:

1. Does this serve a **long run of returning**? If no, push back hard.
2. Does this **punish the user for being human**? If yes, redesign or cut.
3. Does this use **familiar vocabulary** in service of a better mechanic?
4. Does this measure the **user's life**, or our engagement metrics? It should measure the user's life.
5. Could a **Stoic, a Zen practitioner, a craftsperson, and a parent** all nod?
6. Does this feel like the actual app: warm, soft, illustrated, emotionally safe, and forgiving?

When in doubt: **pragmatism over purity — but never at the cost of reintroducing the punishment-reset.** That one mechanic is the line we don't cross, because crossing it makes us the thing we exist to escape.
